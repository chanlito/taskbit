# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Taskbit is for product and developer teams who need one practical place to see issues, tasks, release checks, integration signals, and product feedback before work turns into execution. The primary user is a team member moving between planning, triage, and delivery, often with context scattered across issue trackers, feedback channels, API integration notes, and desktop workflow checks.

## Product Purpose

Taskbit exists to reduce scattered product work and turn disconnected signals into a clear workflow. Success looks like a team understanding what matters now, why it matters, what is already in motion, and what is ready to ship without chasing updates across tools.

The current product surface includes a React web workspace, a Nitro API foundation, and a Tauri desktop shell that opens directly into the app preview. Marketing pages explain the product direction, while the app route should feel like the operational center of the product.

## Brand Personality

Practical, focused, credible.

The brand should feel calm and useful rather than promotional. It should speak like a product team that understands delivery pressure, not like a generic SaaS launch page. Copy should be specific, concise, and honest about placeholder or planned functionality.

## Anti-references

Do not make Taskbit look like a starter template, a hype-heavy SaaS page, or a decorative dashboard mockup with fake analytics. Avoid dark-mode-by-default productivity cliches, purple-blue gradient surfaces, glassy cards, hero metric templates, identical icon card grids, and claims that imply live integrations or production usage before those systems exist.

## Design Principles

1. Make scattered work legible.
   Surfaces should help teams see the relationship between signals, tasks, decisions, and release readiness quickly.

2. Preserve honest product state.
   Placeholder content can feel realistic, but it must not imply live customer data, connected tools, signup flows, or completed integrations.

3. Separate marketing from operation.
   Public pages can explain the product, but the app surface should feel like a focused workspace with its own navigation and density.

4. Prefer quiet confidence over decoration.
   Use typography, spacing, alignment, restrained color, and strong information structure before reaching for effects.

5. Keep momentum visible.
   The interface should make next attention points easy to scan without making the product feel noisy or urgent by default.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Aim for WCAG 2.1 AA contrast and keyboard-accessible navigation. Use semantic links and buttons, visible focus treatment, readable body copy, and responsive layouts that avoid horizontal overflow in desktop, mobile, and narrow in-app browser panes. Motion should be limited, purposeful, and compatible with reduced-motion preferences.
